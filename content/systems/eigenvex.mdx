## The problem

Most engineers fail FAANG system design interviews not because they can't design — but because they don't know what's being scored. The hiring rubric is invisible to the candidate: each company weighs different signals, probes different failure modes, and grades against criteria you only discover after the rejection email. Preparation, meanwhile, is mostly passive — reading blog posts and watching videos — when the skill being tested is live, adversarial, and verbal.

## The product

Eigenvex is an AI-powered interview simulation platform, now in public beta, that puts candidates in realistic back-and-forth interviews graded against company-calibrated rubrics for Google, Meta, Amazon, and Netflix.

- **Seven interview formats** — 1:1, panel, stress interview, bar-raiser, deep dive, rapid fire, and hiring-manager rounds, so a candidate can rehearse an entire loop, not just one conversation.
- **Four difficulty levels** — from Warm-up to Brutal, matching preparation stage from first-time candidate to staff-level promotion.
- **Company-calibrated rubrics** — feedback mapped to each organization's actual hiring criteria and values, making the invisible scoring visible.
- **300+ real questions** — sourced from engineering blogs and community platforms, not invented toy prompts.
- **Failure-mode analysis** — six scenario classes, including traffic spikes and cascade failures, because system design is scored on how designs break.
- **AI-guided design analysis** — structured walkthroughs from requirements through data models.
- **Speech-to-text** — answer out loud, the way the real interview works.

## The business model

Free — no subscription, ever. Every user gets free AI sessions monthly, and a bring-your-own-key model (Google, Anthropic, or OpenAI) unlocks unlimited practice at roughly $0.05–$0.15 per session. The candidate keeps control of cost and provider; the platform stays accessible to anyone.

## Where it fits the thesis

Eigenvex is [Interview Prep OS](/systems/interview-prep-os) graduated from template to product — the clearest demonstration that the operating-layer pattern ships. The same primitives, productized: memory (question banks, session history, per-company preparation state), orchestration (seven formats × four difficulties simulating a full loop), and governance (the rubric itself — explicit, calibrated scoring instead of vibes). It's also the pattern I apply everywhere: find a high-stakes process people treat as folklore, and give it an operating layer.
