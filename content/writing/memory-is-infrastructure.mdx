Ask anyone who works seriously with AI what their biggest frustration is. It's rarely the model's reasoning. It's that yesterday's reasoning is gone.

The idea you refined across a two-hour session. The workflow that finally worked. The context you painstakingly assembled about your codebase, your customers, your constraints. Tomorrow you'll rebuild it, slightly worse, from scratch.

## The tax nobody itemizes

Context loss is a tax levied on every knowledge worker, every team, every enterprise — and it never appears on any balance sheet. We notice compute costs and API bills. We don't notice that our most expensive asset, accumulated understanding, depreciates to zero every time a session ends, a tool switches, or a person leaves.

Organizations feel this most acutely. An enterprise is, functionally, a memory system with people attached: institutional knowledge, decision precedents, hard-won operational intuition. Yet most of it lives in inboxes and heads. When enterprises adopt AI without solving memory, they scale the _forgetting_ along with the thinking.

## Feature vs. infrastructure

Most AI products treat memory as a feature: a toggle, a "remembers your preferences" bullet point. Infrastructure is different. Infrastructure is what everything else silently depends on — plumbing, not furniture.

Treating memory as infrastructure means:

- **Structure over transcripts.** Raw logs aren't memory. Memory is distilled: decisions, rationale, procedures, and their relationships — retrievable by meaning, not by scroll position.
- **Layers with different jobs.** Working memory for the current task, episodic memory for what happened, semantic memory for what's true, procedural memory for what works. Collapse them into one blob and retrieval degrades into noise.
- **Compounding as the design goal.** The test of a memory system isn't recall accuracy; it's whether task N+1 is cheaper than task N. If cost stays flat, you have storage, not memory.

## Why this is the durable bet

Model context windows will keep growing, and people will keep declaring memory solved. It won't be. A bigger window is a bigger desk, not a filing system — it holds more, but organizes nothing, and it still empties at the end of the day.

The compounding curve belongs to whoever builds the layer that persists, structures, and retrieves. Intelligence is becoming abundant. _Remembered_ intelligence is still scarce.
